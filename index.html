The Toll-Booths of the Chip Stack

Global trade statistics measure how much moves through the semiconductor supply chain. They say almost nothing about who owns the chokepoints. This working paper maps the chain as a six-layer network and finds that the real structural power sits with a small number of corporations, not states.

I · The Evolution

From the integrated giant to the hyper-specialised web

The semiconductor industry did not start out distributed. Early Intel designed, fabricated, and packaged its own chips end to end — an integrated device manufacturer (IDM) with sovereign control of its own supply chain. Over four decades that model fractured into dozens of hyper-specialised firms, each holding a narrow, defensible position in one layer of a six-stage global chain.

That fracture created enormous value — and enormous concentration. Nvidia alone is worth more than the GDP of every country in this dataset combined. TSMC and ASML, meanwhile, sit at valuations that dwarf the countries whose trade statistics economists actually track.

Nvidia's market cap: ≈$5.1T. Combined DVA of every country in this network: ≈$0.9T.

II · The Reality of Monopolisation

Trade volume hides power. Concentration reveals it.

Gross trade statistics double-count. A wafer that crosses three borders on its way from sand to smartphone appears three times in the numbers — inflating the apparent diversity of the supply base and obscuring how narrow it actually is at each stage.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index corrects for this by measuring concentration directly — the sum of squared export shares within a layer. One layer spikes into outright monopoly territory: Raw Extraction, where China alone accounts for 72% of source-side flow, chiefly primary gallium. Nothing else comes close.

Equipment and Design & IP look dispersed by this measure — but that undercounts the truth. Both are led by the United States at only ~21–22% of source share, yet dominated in substance by a small handful of US-headquartered firms whose combined national portfolio is a far tighter chokepoint than the country-level number implies.

III · Graph Theory in Plain English

Think of it as a highway system, not a trade ledger

Picture the supply chain as a system of international highways. We are not measuring how much cargo moves on them. We are measuring who owns the toll-booth on the shortest route between any two points.

Betweenness centrality counts, for every possible origin and destination, how many of the shortest paths between them are forced through a given node. A node with high betweenness is a highway interchange: close it, and traffic across the entire system backs up. That is why we call it the toll-booth parameter — it is a direct measure of how much rent a node can extract, and how catastrophic its disruption would be.

III·B · Network Analysis, as a Secondary Layer

What the topology adds once the conventional numbers are on the table

Two supporting tests, run against the same 47-node network. First: what happens if you actually remove the chokepoints? Targeted removal of the highest-betweenness node, recalculated at every step, collapses the network's giant component below half its size after just 17% of nodes are gone. Random failure — the kind trade shocks and natural disasters usually cause — takes removing 47% of nodes to do the same damage. That gap is the fragility the valuation chart hints at, made explicit.

Second: power is not held the same way by every actor. Using a multiplex participation coefficient, we can separate nodes that are powerful in one layer only from nodes that are powerful across several. Almost every marquee firm — ASML, TSMC, Arm, Cadence/Synopsys — is a pure single-layer specialist: enormous depth, zero cross-layer reach. China is the one outlier with genuine breadth, active across four of six layers at once.
