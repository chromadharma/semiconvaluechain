The Toll-Booths of the Chip Stack

Trade statistics count how many chips cross a border. They are silent on who could stop them from crossing at all. This paper rebuilds the global semiconductor supply chain as a six-layer network and finds its chokepoints held not by states, but by a couple of dozen firms most people have never heard of.

I · The Evolution

From the integrated giant to the hyper-specialised web

Intel used to do everything itself: design a chip, etch it, package it, ship it. That vertical empire has since splintered into a supply chain so specialised that no single firm could rebuild it alone even if it wanted to — each link defensible precisely because it does the one thing nobody else can.

Splintering paid extraordinarily well. Nvidia is now worth more than the combined economic output of every country in this network. TSMC and ASML — a foundry and a lithography firm most of the public still couldn't place on a map — are worth more than the trade flows of the nations whose GDP economists spend their careers tracking.

Nvidia's market cap: ≈$5.1T. Combined DVA of every country in this network: ≈$0.9T.

II · The Reality of Monopolisation

Trade volume hides power. Concentration reveals it.

Gross trade figures have a counting problem. A wafer that crosses three borders between sand and smartphone gets counted three times, and every crossing makes the supply base look a little more diverse than it actually is.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index — economists' usual instrument for spotting a monopoly — cuts through that. One layer scores as an outright cartel: Raw Extraction, where China supplies 72% of everything that moves, almost entirely primary gallium. Nothing else in the chain comes close.

Equipment and Design & IP score as competitive on the same measure, led by the United States at a modest 21–22%. Don't be fooled. That leading country is a short, familiar list of American firms in disguise — and their combined grip is far tighter than any single national statistic lets on.

III · Graph Theory in Plain English

A map of motorways, not a trade ledger

Picture the supply chain as a map of motorways rather than a ledger of cargo. The question worth asking isn't how much traffic moves — it's who owns the tollbooth that traffic has no way around.

Betweenness centrality answers exactly that: for every possible origin and destination, what share of shortest routes between them has no choice but to pass through a given node. Score high, and you are not a supplier among many — you are the interchange. Shut you down, and the system doesn't reroute. It stops.

III·B · Network Analysis, as a Secondary Layer

What the topology adds once you've seen the price tags

Two stress tests on the same 47-node network make the case harder to dismiss as valuation theatre. Pull out the highest-betweenness node, recompute, repeat — and the giant component falls below half its size after losing just 17% of nodes. Force the same collapse at random, the way an earthquake or a shipping crisis would, and it takes 47%. That gap is the entire argument for why chokepoints matter more than any diversification slide suggests.

Power, it turns out, isn't distributed evenly even among the powerful. A multiplex participation score separates nodes that matter in one layer from nodes that matter everywhere. ASML, TSMC, Arm, Cadence and Synopsys are all the former — immense within their lane, invisible outside it. Only one node in the network is powerful almost everywhere at once: China, active across four of six layers. Depth is common. Breadth is not.

“Depth is common. Breadth is not.”
